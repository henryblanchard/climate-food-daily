## 1. Climate-tech agrifood investment recovered to $3.9B

After a brutal 2024, climate-focused agrifoodtech funding climbed back from $2.8B to roughly $3.9B in 2025, according to AgFunder's latest report. South Korea was the surprise standout, growing the category by 171%. Overall agrifoodtech sat almost flat at $16.2B, but capital is concentrating into companies with real science and visible paths to revenue.

*Why this matters:* the funding "ice age" narrative is softening, but only for startups that can show unit economics, not promises. Expect more selectivity in any climate-adjacent ag round you see this year.

[AgFunder: agrifoodtech funding flat, but the story is shifting](https://agfundernews.com/agrifoodtech-funding-is-flat-the-interesting-part-is-where-it-isnt)

## 2. Japan turns methane-cutting cattle feed into carbon credits

Japan's J-Credit scheme has approved a methodology that lets livestock producers earn carbon credits for feeding cattle methane-reducing additives. dsm-firmenich's Bovaer is now in a feeding trial with Wagyu beef cattle, on the back of approvals showing 30% methane reduction in dairy cows and up to 45% in beef.

*Why this matters:* this is the first time a major economy has plugged feed-additive methane reduction directly into a tradable credit market: a meaningful template for the EU and US to follow, and a real revenue line for ranchers.

[AgTech Navigator: Japan approves methane-cutting feed additive under J-Credit](https://www.agtechnavigator.com/Article/2026/02/25/japan-approves-methane-cutting-feed-additive-under-j-credit-scheme/)

## 3. Ruminant BioTech raises $9.5M for a slow-release methane bolus

New Zealand's Ruminant BioTech closed a NZD $17M ($9.5M USD) Series A to launch a slow-release bolus designed to cut methane in *grazing* cattle, where most feed additives fail because farmers can't dose animals daily on open pasture.

*Why this matters:* feed additives only solve part of the methane problem. If a single bolus actually works for months on extensive grazing systems, it unlocks the largest emitting tier of global beef production.

[AgFunder News: Ruminant BioTech nets $9.5m for slow-release bolus](https://agfundernews.com/can-a-slow-release-bolus-crack-methane-reduction-for-pasture-raised-cattle-ruminant-biotech-nets-9-5m)

## 4. Cultivated meat enters 2026 leaner, and finally honest

After 2025 wiped out high-profile names including Meatable and Believer Meats, cultivated protein funding fell 48% to $74M. But the survivors are recalibrating around hybrids, pet food, and pharma-style bioprocess work rather than mass-market burgers.

*Why this matters:* the hype cycle is over and the engineering era is starting. The next wins will look more like "cell-cultured ingredient inside a plant-based product" than "cultivated steak on a plate", which is a quieter but more investable shape.

[Protein Production Technology: cultivated meat in 2026, fewer illusions, firmer footing](https://www.proteinproductiontechnology.com/post/cultivated-meat-in-2026-fewer-illusions-firmer-footing)

## 5. Vertical farming hits $7.5B and stops apologising for itself

The vertical farming market reached $7.5B in 2026, with operators finally talking about real revenue and unit-level profit instead of bold press releases. AI-driven yield forecasting, autonomous seeding, and real-time environmental control have shifted from pilot to baseline. Geographic centre of gravity is widening to Southeast Asia, the Gulf, and Northern Europe.

*Why this matters:* the sector survived its near-death moment by getting boring; automation is the actual moat. Watch container farms in remote, import-dependent geographies, where vertical economics work in ways they don't in temperate-climate suburbs.

[AGEYE: vertical farming market hit $7.5B](https://ageyetech.com/news/vertical-farming-market-size-2026)

## 6. Debt financing hits a decade high in agrifoodtech

Debt and late-stage instruments are now 18.2% of total agrifood funding, the highest share in 10 years. Names like Chestnut Carbon, Cambrian Innovation, and Samunnati raised through structured debt rather than equity dilution, especially in carbon, water, and infrastructure plays.

*Why this matters:* if you're a founder in a hardware-heavy or project-finance-friendly part of climate ag, equity isn't your only lane any more. If you're an LP, your "agrifood VC" exposure is increasingly *not* in VC funds.

[AgFunder research: agrifoodtech funding by stage](https://agfunder.com/research/)
