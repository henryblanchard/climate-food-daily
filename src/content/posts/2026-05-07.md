## 1. Denmark's Bovaer mandate is in crisis, EFSA opinion due June 30

Denmark became the world's first country to require a methane-reducing feed additive for dairy herds, but the rollout is faltering. About 400 of the 1,600 farms that began feeding Bovaer (dsm-firmenich's 3-NOP product) in late 2024 have reported health issues including crashed milk yields, digestive disorders, and in some cases animal deaths. EFSA launched a formal safety reassessment after collecting field data through a March 31, 2026 submission deadline, with its scientific opinion now due June 30. Norway and Sweden have already paused their own Bovaer trials while the review proceeds.

*Why this matters:* whether EFSA greenlights, restricts, or pulls Bovaer will shape the entire regulatory template for methane additives in EU livestock systems, with knock-on consequences for every feed company, carbon programme, and farmer who has bet on this pathway.

## 2. Halter raises $220M at $2B valuation to take virtual fencing global

New Zealand-based Halter, which makes solar-powered AI cow collars for virtual fencing and pasture management, closed a $220M Series E in March led by Founders Fund, doubling its valuation to $2 billion in under a year. The company has now sold over one million collars, serves more than 2,000 farmers across New Zealand, Australia, and the US, and plans to expand into Ireland and the UK later in 2026. Founders Fund's Peter Thiel flagged the recurring-revenue model and real-time animal health monitoring as key drivers of the bet.

*Why this matters:* the Founders Fund backing signals that premium livestock hardware is finally being valued on subscription logic rather than ag-tech promise, and the Ireland/UK expansion brings Halter to Europe's most financially stretched pastoral farming sector at precisely the moment that the Bovaer controversy is sharpening demand for non-feed-additive climate tools.

## 3. MicroHarvest's microbial protein lands in UK pet food aisles

Hamburg-based MicroHarvest is on course to have its fermentation-derived protein ingredient inside 15 European pet food products by the end of Q2 2026. British brand THE PACK has already launched the UK's first microbial protein dog treats, called Gut Bites, using MicroHarvest's ingredient, which is produced in a 24-hour fermentation cycle on regional agri-food side streams. The company is developing a 15,000-tonne-per-year production facility in Leuna, Germany, targeting completion by end of 2027.

*Why this matters:* pet food is proving to be the clearest commercial entry point for novel fermentation proteins, with lower regulatory hurdles and more premium price tolerance than human food, and MicroHarvest's 15-product pipeline points to genuine market pull rather than lab-to-shelf hope.

## 4. Agreena issues 2.3 million Verra-verified carbon credits for European arable farms

Copenhagen-based Agreena has become the first large-scale arable farming project verified under Verra's Verified Carbon Standard, issuing 2.3 million Verified Carbon Units across 1.6 million hectares of farmland spanning the UK, Denmark, Ukraine, Romania, Spain, and several other European countries. The credits represent nearly 1.2 million tonnes of CO2 removed through regenerative practices including cover cropping, reduced tillage, and precision nutrient management, verified using AI-driven digital MRV and satellite imagery.

*Why this matters:* Verra certification addresses the quality objection that has dogged soil carbon credits since 2022, and a project at 1.6 million hectares gives corporate buyers the volume they need for material Scope 3 claims without aggregating thousands of small farm deals individually.

[Carbon Credits: AgreenaCarbon's 2.3 Million Verified Carbon Credits Redefine Regenerative Agriculture](https://carboncredits.com/scaling-sustainable-farming-agreenacarbons-2-3-million-verified-carbon-credits-redefine-regenerative-agriculture/)

## 5. Symbrosia files with FDA and plans a 15-acre Hawaii seaweed facility

Hawaii-based Symbrosia has submitted a data package to the FDA seeking approval for SeaGraze, its red seaweed livestock feed additive, targeting a mid-2026 regulatory decision. The company, which raised $5.8M in a Series A-1 round, is also planning construction of a 15-acre cultivation facility in Kona, with offtake agreements already covering 88% of planned output. Financing will draw on USDA-backed government-guaranteed debt, aligned with BioPreferred programme criteria.

*Why this matters:* US approval of a seaweed-based methane additive would give American ranchers a biologically distinct alternative to synthetic 3-NOP products at a moment when the Bovaer controversy is intensifying demand for options beyond one incumbent technology.

## 6. Nature Food maps a decade of alternative protein and sets the terms for the next ten years

A new review in Nature Food traces the alternative protein sector from its 2021 investment peak through its retrenchment, and identifies what the next decade actually requires. The paper flags regulatory fragmentation across jurisdictions, bioprocess scale-up costs, and limited consumer acceptance of insect and algae categories as three structural bottlenecks. Plant-based meats rank highest for near-term viability, given established production infrastructure and growing retailer commitment. Precision fermentation proteins are described as 5-8 years from meaningful food-system penetration.

*Why this matters:* for investors setting portfolio timelines, the paper is a useful recalibration: the evidence-backed bet right now is plant-based infrastructure and precision fermentation speciality ingredients, not cultivated bulk protein at commodity scale.

[Nature Food: The next decade of alternative proteins](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43016-026-01344-x)
