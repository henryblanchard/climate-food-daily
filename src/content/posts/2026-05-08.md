## 1. AgZen closes $10M Series B as AI precision spray crosses two million acres

Massachusetts-based AgZen has closed a $10 million Series B led by DCVC Bio, with participation from Astanor Ventures, Material Impact, and Syngenta Group Ventures. The company's RealCoverage system uses computer vision and real-time AI to adjust droplet size, spray angle, and application rate on the fly, slashing pesticide and herbicide use. In a single commercial season, deployed acreage expanded 15x to nearly one million acres in the US. For 2026, AgZen has already signed commitments covering more than two million acres across three continents. Growers report average chemical savings of up to 50%, with payback typically under a year.

*Why this matters:* Syngenta co-investing alongside a precision spray startup that cuts agrochemical volumes is a signal worth watching: either the major ag-chem houses are buying optionality on disruption or, more bullishly, they see precision application as a tool that unlocks premium chemistry at lower total-cost-to-grower.

## 2. Iceland's Thor Salmon bets on volcanic water filtration as a salmon farming moat

A feature published this week by Ag-Tech Navigator profiles Iceland's emerging cluster of land-based salmon farms, with Thor Salmon as the centrepiece. Located near Thorlakshofn in southern Iceland, the facility is licensed for 20,000 tonnes annually, with expansion plans to 40,000 tonnes. The site draws on groundwater that has been naturally filtered through volcanic lava rock, providing a unique mix of fresh and salt water at optimal temperature. Thor Salmon has installed Innovasea's Gas Management Tower technology, cutting energy costs by up to 77% versus pressurised oxygen systems. The company has raised $31 million from Icelandic and international investors.

*Why this matters:* Iceland's volcanic geology offers a rare combination of energy cost advantage and natural water quality that is genuinely hard to replicate elsewhere; if Thor Salmon can execute at 40,000 tonnes, it reframes land-based Atlantic salmon not as a marginal premium product but a volume play with structural cost advantages over sea-pen rivals in Scotland and Norway.

## 3. Fudi Protein attracts Green Boy Group backing for alfalfa-derived RuBisCO

Green Boy Group, a global plant-based ingredient supplier, led an early-stage investment in Fudi Protein on April 30, becoming the startup's first institutional backer. Fudi extracts RuBisCO, the protein found in every green leaf and the most abundant enzyme on Earth, from regenerative alfalfa using a proprietary low-solvent process. The resulting ingredient is colourless, flavourless, and functions as a 1:1 replacement for egg white protein in baking and manufacturing applications. The company was founded by Udi Lazimy, previously of Eat Just, and is targeting US food manufacturers seeking cost-stable egg alternatives amid ongoing supply disruptions from avian influenza.

*Why this matters:* RuBisCO from alfalfa is one of the few novel proteins that stacks a regenerative-ag story (alfalfa fixes nitrogen, improves soil structure) on top of functional performance, and the egg-replacement angle gives it a concrete near-term revenue hook that most novel proteins lack.

## 4. Oberland Agriscience ships commercial-scale insect protein from Nova Scotia

Canadian black soldier fly larvae startup Oberland Agriscience has appointed food industry veteran Jon Getzinger as CEO, with founder Greg Wanger moving to CTO, as the company transitions from commissioning to full-scale operations. Oberland has been running at commercial scale since July 2025, shipping truckloads of BSFL protein and frass primarily to US customers, and expects to reach full licensed capacity in Q1 or early Q2 2026. Getzinger has previously held senior roles at PURIS, DSM, Cargill, and NutraSweet, signalling an intent to professionalize sales and route-to-market.

*Why this matters:* while European BSFL players including Protix and Hermetia have dominated coverage, Oberland's North American footprint and US-bound revenue gives it a distribution advantage at a moment when US feed and pet food buyers are actively seeking domestic supply of alternative protein ingredients.

## 5. UK FSA publishes clearer approval pathway for precision and biomass fermentation

The UK's Food Standards Agency and Food Standards Scotland published updated novel food authorisation guidance in March 2026, with a dedicated supplementary framework for products derived from precision fermentation and biomass fermentation. The update includes a new pre-application business support pilot, a refreshed guidance hub, and clarified timelines. The FSA's own data show authorisations have averaged 2.5 years against a statutory target of 17 months; the new guidance is designed to front-load the science review and reduce back-and-forth. The FSA identified fermentation-derived proteins, controlled-environment agriculture ingredients, and cell-cultivated products as the four categories closest to market release.

*Why this matters:* the UK has positioned itself as the most commercially legible regulatory environment for novel proteins outside Singapore; clearer timelines and a pre-application support service lower the compliance cost that has pushed several precision fermentation companies to prioritise the US first.

## 6. Niqo Robotics targets profitability in FY2026/27 as AI weeder expands to 15 crops

Indian-founded, California-operating ag robotics company Niqo Robotics says its core business is on track for profitability in its first full commercial year, a milestone no crop-robotics company has publicly claimed. The company's RoboWeeder, built around its proprietary Niqo Sense AI camera system, has expanded from lettuce to more than 15 specialty crops including onions, tomatoes, broccoli, kale, and melons. Niqo is entering the Pacific Northwest and targeting Europe and Australia for international expansion, while a next-generation robot built for a wider range of US field configurations is due in the second half of 2026.

*Why this matters:* ag robotics has been a graveyard of overfunded promises; a company credibly claiming profitability without a headline funding round shifts the conversation from "when will this work" to "which product wins", and Niqo's crop-expansion strategy suggests a platform play rather than a single-crop niche.
