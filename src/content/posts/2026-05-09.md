## 1. Rumin8 and Minerva Foods Cut Cattle Methane 50% in Brazilian Feedlot Trial

Australian biotech Rumin8, in partnership with Brazilian beef producer Minerva Foods, published results on May 8 showing a 50.4% reduction in enteric methane from Nelore cattle over a 120-day feedlot study conducted with ESALQ/USP. Animals receiving the synthetic bromoform-based feed additive dropped from 77.2g of methane per kilogram of live weight gain to 39.6g, and showed a 5% improvement in feed conversion efficiency. The trial covered 280 animals across individual and collective pens to simulate both measurement-precision and commercial-scale conditions. The trial is estimated to have avoided 29.8 tonnes of CO2 equivalent, with independent verification now underway by Athian and FoodChain ID.

*Why this matters:* Brazil holds the world's largest commercial cattle herd and accounts for roughly a quarter of global beef exports, so a validated, scalable methane additive in this geography represents one of the highest-leverage decarbonisation opportunities in the food system.

## 2. Fermeate Raises $2M Seed to Use Light as a Fermentation Dial

California startup Fermeate closed a $2 million seed round led by Newfund Capital, with SOSV, Ajinomoto Group Ventures, Ki Tua Fund and others participating. The company uses optogenetics, originally developed for neuroscience, to control microbial gene expression in real time via targeted light pulses. The approach improves fermentation outputs by 60% to 300% without replacing existing stainless-steel tanks; the retrofit costs less than 5% of the price of new capacity, with payback periods under 11 months based on third-party technoeconomic analysis. Fermeate has already achieved up to a 200% increase in protein production in pilots with four global food and ingredient companies.

*Why this matters:* Precision fermentation's path to cost parity with conventional protein depends on squeezing more yield from existing infrastructure rather than building expensive new facilities; Fermeate's retrofit model could reopen investment cases that stalled on capital intensity.

## 3. Friends and Family Pet Food Brings Cultivated Quail Meat to Singapore Shelves

US startup Friends and Family Pet Food launched 12 SKUs of cultivated quail meat treats and toppers for cats and dogs into retail in Singapore on April 28, following a sold-out debut at the Singapore Pet Expo. Products are freeze-dried, carry up to nearly 70% cultivated meat content, and span four functional lines: Pure Protein, Digestive Balance, Teeth and Gum Care, and Skin and Coat Glow. They are the first cultivated-meat pet food products sold in retail in Asia and the first-ever cultivated products formulated specifically for cats. The launch follows Singapore's Animal and Veterinary Service regulatory approval granted in 2025, through an exclusive partnership with premium retailer Vanillapup.

*Why this matters:* The pet food channel is proving to be cultivated meat's lowest-friction path to retail, sidestepping the consumer scepticism and labeling disputes that continue to stall human food applications in most markets.

## 4. FAO and WMO: Every Degree of Warming Cuts Maize Yields by 7.5%

A joint report from the UN Food and Agriculture Organisation and the World Meteorological Organization, "Extreme Heat and Agriculture," quantifies that maize yields have declined 7.5% per degree Celsius of warming, with wheat yields down 6%. The report finds extreme heat now threatens the livelihoods of over a billion agricultural workers and rural communities, hitting smallholder farmers in tropical regions hardest. Heat stress is simultaneously depressing dairy milk output and compressing peak-season labour productivity across the Global South.

*Why this matters:* These per-degree loss coefficients will become a standard input for commodity price models, crop insurance actuaries, and climate-smart seed developers pricing adaptation risk into product pipelines.

[WMO: Extreme heat pushes agrifood systems to the brink](https://wmo.int/news/media-centre/extreme-heat-pushes-agrifood-systems-brink)

## 5. Academic Labs Fill the Scale-Up Gap That Alt-Protein Capital Left Behind

A Nutraingredients analysis published May 1 identifies universities as a critical bridge in the alternative protein commercialisation pipeline, providing pilot-scale and scale-up infrastructure that most startups cannot fund on their own. The University of Illinois' iFAB Tech Hub and the Plant Protein Innovation Center at the University of Minnesota are cited as examples of publicly funded capacity now being used to derisk precision fermentation and plant protein processes before private capital commits. This matters because overall alt-protein investment remains near multi-year lows, with roughly $383 million raised across 28 deals in 2025, a 65% fall from 2024.

*Why this matters:* Founders and investors should map academic scale-up assets alongside commercial co-manufacturing partners when building capital-efficient commercialisation plans in the current funding environment.

[Nutraingredients: Universities bridge gaps in alternative protein scale-up and commercialization](https://www.nutraingredients.com/Article/2026/05/01/universities-bridge-gaps-in-alternative-protein-scale-up-and-commercialization/)
