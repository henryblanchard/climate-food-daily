## 1. FDA Proposes to Scrap Self-Affirmed GRAS, Reshaping Novel Ingredient Access

The US FDA has placed a proposed rule on its spring 2026 agenda that would eliminate the self-affirmed Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) pathway, requiring companies to submit mandatory pre-market safety notifications for all food and animal-feed ingredients. Under the current voluntary system, companies can introduce substances to market after completing an internal safety review without notifying the FDA at all. The proposed change would convert that into a compulsory pre-clearance process, with ingredients already listed as GRAS by regulation or covered by an existing FDA "no questions" letter grandfathered in. Legal experts note the agency may lack statutory authority to mandate notifications, which would invite court challenges; but even a proposed rule creates compliance uncertainty that freezes ingredient pipelines.

*Why this matters:* For precision fermentation startups and ag biotech companies targeting the US market, the FDA "no questions" letter has just become the only durable route to commercial launch, raising entry barriers and extending timelines across the sector.

## 2. Verley Closes $38M Series A to Bring Precision-Fermented Whey to the US

French ingredient startup Verley has raised $38 million in an oversubscribed Series A to commercialize beta-lactoglobulin (BLG), a functional whey protein produced through precision fermentation without dairy animals. The round comprised $30 million in equity from Alven, Blast, Sofinnova Partners, Captech Sante, Sparkfood, and Founders Future, alongside $8 million in non-dilutive support from French national investment bank Bpifrance. Verley achieved self-affirmed GRAS status in 2024 and secured an FDA "no questions" letter in 2025, clearing the way for its planned US commercial launch later in 2026. The company is targeting sports nutrition and infant formula rather than positioning BLG as a commodity dairy replacement.

*Why this matters:* Verley's sequential FDA clearance and immediate US expansion is a credible proof point for European precision fermentation investors who have long questioned whether the US regulatory path is navigable; it also lands just as the FDA's GRAS overhaul makes that clearance significantly more valuable.

## 3. MicroHarvest Backs Leuna Facility with German Federal Grant and Lines Up 15 Pet Food Launches

Hamburg-based MicroHarvest is building a 15,000-tonne-per-year biomass fermentation plant in Leuna, Germany, backed by a 5.46 million euro grant from Germany's Federal Funding Programme for Energy and Resource Efficiency in Industry. The facility uses a 24-hour fermentation cycle that converts regional agri-food side streams into a hypoallergenic protein ingredient with a fraction of the carbon footprint of conventional meat protein. Alongside the infrastructure build, MicroHarvest confirmed more than 15 new pet food product launches scheduled for Q2 2026, extending commercial traction that already spans over 1,800 German retail locations. Full production capacity at Leuna is targeted for the end of 2027.

*Why this matters:* Pet food is emerging as the revenue anchor that de-risks biomass fermentation businesses while human food applications scale; German federal backing for Leuna signals that biomanufacturing infrastructure is being treated as industrial policy, not just venture speculation.

[Source: Protein Production Technology International, "MicroHarvest plants its industrial flag in Leuna with 15,000-ton fermentation facility"](https://www.proteinproductiontechnology.com/post/microharvest-plants-its-industrial-flag-in-leuna-with-15-000-ton-fermentation-facility-and-eu5-46m-federal-backing)

## 4. PARIMA Becomes First International Cultivated Meat Company to Enter FSANZ Scientific Review

PARIMA, the company formed from the merger of French startups Gourmey and Vital Meat, has had its cultivated duck dossier formally accepted by Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ) for full scientific risk assessment. The application, filed under the Gourmey brand name, seeks to permit cell-cultured duck as a food ingredient for products including duck pate and cultivated foie gras analogs, produced from Pekin duck embryonic stem cells at concentrations of 5 to 80 percent of finished product weight. FSANZ classed it as a General Level 5 procedure, involving one public consultation round, with a decision expected by late August 2026. This makes PARIMA the first international cultivated meat company to reach formal scientific review in Australia or New Zealand.

*Why this matters:* Australia and New Zealand offer cultivated meat companies a credible Western regulatory proving ground outside the US and Singapore; a positive FSANZ outcome would give European firms a concrete third-market pathway and add pressure on slower-moving EU regulators.

## 5. Agreena Issues 2.3 Million Verra-Verified Carbon Units from European Arable Farms

Denmark-based Agreena has achieved registration of the first large-scale arable agriculture project under Verra's Verified Carbon Standard, issuing 2.3 million Verified Carbon Units (VCUs) covering vintage years 2021 to 2023. The programme spans 4.5 million hectares farmed by 2,300 growers across 20 countries, from Ukraine to Spain, with practice changes including cover cropping, reduced tillage, and optimised fertilizer application. Agreena uses AI-driven digital measurement, reporting, and verification (dMRV) calibrated against ground samples, with over 1.1 million tonnes of CO2 now recorded as stored in soils. Corporate buyers can purchase credits through Agreena's direct channel, with Verra's registry providing independent third-party assurance.

*Why this matters:* Cropland soil carbon has historically been viewed as a lower-integrity credit category than forestry or peatlands; Agreena's Verra certification under a rigorous methodology sets a benchmark that could unlock demand from corporate buyers previously unwilling to engage with agricultural soil offsets.

[Source: Carbon Credits, "Scaling Sustainable Farming: AgreenaCarbon's 2.3 Million Verified Carbon Credits Redefine Regenerative Agriculture"](https://carboncredits.com/scaling-sustainable-farming-agreenacarbons-2-3-million-verified-carbon-credits-redefine-regenerative-agriculture/)

## 6. Satellites Are Reshaping How Europe Prices and Verifies Soil Carbon

Satellite imagery is becoming central to how European soil carbon credits are monitored, with earth observation data now allowing developers to track practice changes such as cover cropping and reduced tillage across thousands of farms without relying solely on field visits. Satellite-based verification is estimated to cut MRV costs by up to 40 percent compared to traditional soil sampling, and analysts project that 90 percent of European credit transactions will require satellite verification by 2027. The shift is accelerating in parallel with the EU's Carbon Removal and Carbon Farming Regulation (CRCF), which published its first permanent removal methodologies in February 2026 and is expected to adopt agricultural and agroforestry methodologies later this year. One key constraint remains: satellites cannot measure soil carbon directly underground, meaning ground-truth sampling stays mandatory for regulatory compliance.

*Why this matters:* Cheaper MRV is the critical bottleneck for scaling agricultural carbon credits in Europe; if the satellite-ground hybrid model becomes the CRCF standard, it changes the unit economics for mid-sized operators who previously could not justify the cost of traditional certification.
