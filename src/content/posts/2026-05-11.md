## 1. Standing Ovation Raises $34.2M to Bring Precision-Fermented Casein to the US

Paris-based Standing Ovation has closed a €30 million ($34.2M) Series B to commercialise its precision-fermented casein protein, targeting a US commercial launch before year-end. The round was led by Bpifrance's Ecotechnologies 2 fund and Crédit Mutuel Innovation, with Danone Ventures joining existing backer Bel Group as shareholders. The company's technology converts acid whey, a low-value cheese-making by-product, into casein using engineered microorganisms, resolving a dairy waste problem while producing a high-value protein. An FDA GRAS submission is expected by end of 2026, with an EFSA novel foods filing to follow.

*Why this matters:* Backing from two of Europe's largest dairy companies, plus a clear waste-stream feedstock, gives Standing Ovation a route to industrial scale and strategic buyers that most fermentation startups lack.

## 2. Perfect Day Says Its Gujarat Whey Plant Will Be Profitable from Day One

Perfect Day's precision fermentation facility in Bharuch, Gujarat, a joint venture with Indian pharma group Zydus Lifesciences, remains on track for initial operations in the second half of 2026 with a ramp-up in 2027. The plant will produce beta-lactoglobulin (BLG), a functional whey protein, without any dairy animals. Perfect Day's COO told AgFunderNews the company is "confident in instant profitability at plant launch," citing signed commercial contracts and a fully sold-out initial capacity. The facility targets India's growing sports nutrition and functional dairy markets while positioning the company to compete on cost with conventional whey producers.

*Why this matters:* Day-one profitability at a first-of-kind precision fermentation facility in a major emerging market would remove the most persistent investor objection to the sector's commercial viability.

## 3. Japan Plugs Methane-Reducing Feed Additives into Its National Carbon Credit System

Japan's J-Credit Scheme approved a new methodology in February 2026 that allows cattle producers to earn tradeable carbon credits by using enteric methane-reducing feed additives, including Bovaer (3-NOP), made by dsm-firmenich. Bovaer, which received Japanese market approval in late 2024, cuts enteric methane by roughly 30% in dairy cows and 45% in beef cattle. The integration links a commercial agricultural input directly to carbon income for producers, creating a financial incentive on top of the regulatory permission that already exists. Japan's move aligns with its MIDORI agricultural sustainability strategy and sets a precedent other major beef and dairy markets are likely to study.

*Why this matters:* Japan is the first major Asian economy to tie a livestock feed additive to a national carbon crediting framework, demonstrating how regulatory approval and carbon markets can be sequenced to drive real-world adoption.

## 4. MiAlgae Brings Its Whisky-Waste Omega-3 Plant Online at Grangemouth

Scottish biotech MiAlgae is commissioning its major scale-up facility in Grangemouth this quarter, after breaking ground in December 2025. The plant, backed by up to £3M in joint UK and Scottish government investment under the Project Willow industrial regeneration scheme, will expand MiAlgae's algal DHA output more than tenfold. The production process feeds microalgae on Scotch whisky distillery by-products, diverting 36 million litres of liquid waste per year while replacing marine-sourced omega-3 oils used in pet food and aquaculture feed. At full scale, MiAlgae estimates the facility will save the equivalent of six billion wild fish annually from being harvested for fishmeal and oil.

*Why this matters:* With marine omega-3 supply under strain from overfishing and climate-driven stock shifts, algal production at this scale gives aquaculture and pet food manufacturers a credible traceable alternative, and public funding at Grangemouth signals that governments are treating biotech as industrial infrastructure.

## 5. EU Sets July 2026 Deadline to Decide on Livestock Methane in Its Carbon Certification Framework

The European Commission is working toward a July 2026 decision on whether to extend its Carbon Removal Certification Framework (CRCF) to cover methane from livestock enteric fermentation and manure management, as required by Article 18(3) of Regulation (EU) 2024/3012. DG CLIMA hosted a stakeholder workshop in February 2026 to develop a pilot certification for livestock emission reductions, examining technologies including Bovaer (3-NOP), manure acidification, and anaerobic digestion. Livestock produce 55% of anthropogenic methane in the EU, making the sector critical to meeting the bloc's 2030 climate targets. A positive decision would, for the first time, place agricultural methane inside a recognised EU tradeable certification system.

*Why this matters:* For EU livestock operators, a formal CRCF pathway would unlock carbon revenue that directly offsets the cost of methane mitigation technology, potentially flipping the adoption calculus for feed additives and manure upgrades from cost to net-positive.

## 6. Notpla Wins EUR4M Horizon Europe Grant to Scale Seaweed-Coated Coffee Cups

UK startup Notpla has secured a €4 million grant from the EU's Horizon Europe programme to develop a fully plastic-free, home-compostable coffee cup using its seaweed-based coating technology, working alongside a consortium of 14 partners across Europe and Africa. The project will cover material testing, performance validation, lifecycle assessment, and scalability studies to meet the demands of the global coffee industry. Notpla, which won Prince William's Earthshot Prize in 2022, piloted a first-generation espresso cup in Brazil in 2025 and has already rolled out seaweed-coated food trays into 160 Dutch restaurants via a partnership with Kwalitaria. More than 500 billion disposable coffee cups are discarded globally each year, the majority lined with virgin plastic.

*Why this matters:* Horizon Europe backing gives Notpla the resources to close the gap between pilot and category-scale production for a material that sidesteps both the recyclability problem of paper cups and the cost of biodegradable plastic liners.
