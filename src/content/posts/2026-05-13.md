## 1. Meatly Raises £10.4M Series A to Build Europe's Largest Cultivated Meat Facility

UK startup Meatly has closed a £10.4 million Series A backed by Oyster Bay Venture Capital, Clean Growth Fund, and JamJar Investments, bringing total funding to £17.4 million. The capital funds a 20,000-litre pilot bioreactor facility in London, designed to be the largest cultivated meat site in Europe. Meatly focuses on cultivated chicken for the pet food market, where regulatory hurdles are lower than for human food; production is expected to begin supplying customers in 2027. The round builds on £7 million in seed funding from founding investor Agronomics and Pets at Home.

*Why this matters:* Pet food is proving to be the fastest commercial pathway for cultivated meat in regulated markets, and a 20,000-litre facility would move cost curves in a way that smaller pilot runs cannot.

## 2. Biosphere Wins $9M US Army Contract for Portable Protein-From-Air Bioreactors

California startup Biosphere has been awarded up to $9 million from the US Army Combat Capabilities Development Command (DEVCOM) to develop field-deployable bioreactors that produce protein rations from air, water, and electricity using gas fermentation. The three-and-a-half year programme targets portable units capable of generating 2,800 calories per day for up to 18 soldiers in remote or logistically constrained environments, with a scale-up path to 250 personnel. Biosphere's UV sterilise-in-place protocol cuts material costs tenfold compared with conventional fermentation systems, according to the company.

*Why this matters:* Military procurement is a historically reliable forcing function for frontier biotech; defence-grade validation of portable gas fermentation infrastructure could accelerate civilian applications in off-grid agriculture and humanitarian food production.

## 3. Canada Channels C$30M Into Six Ag-Clean-Tech Accelerators

The Canadian government has allocated up to C$30 million over two years to six not-for-profit organisations under the Agricultural Clean Technology Program's Research and Innovation Stream. The recipients are Bioenterprise Canada, the Canadian Agri-Food Automation and Intelligence Network (CAAIN), the Canadian Food Innovation Network (CFIN), MaRS Discovery District, Ontario Genomics, and Zone Agtech. Each organisation will run its own competitive grant intake, redistributing capital to startups and agri-businesses developing technologies that cut greenhouse gas emissions from Canadian farming.

*Why this matters:* Routing government capital through sector-focused intermediaries rather than direct grants introduces commercial discipline earlier and improves deal quality, a model other governments are watching.

[Source: Canada.ca, "Government of Canada invests $30 million in six not-for-profit organizations to help accelerate clean technology innovation across Canadian agriculture"](https://www.canada.ca/en/agriculture-agri-food/news/2026/05/government-of-canada-invests-30-million-in-six-not-for-profit-organizations-to-help-accelerate-clean-technology-innovation-across-canadian-agriculture.html)

## 4. New Zealand Backs Low-Methane Sheep Genetics With NZ$1.2M Research Extension

AgriZeroNZ, Beef + Lamb New Zealand, the Ag Emissions Centre, and New Zealand's Ministry for Primary Industries have jointly invested NZ$1.2 million to extend the Cool Sheep Programme for another year. The programme will measure methane emissions and collect genetic data from 5,000 animals to sharpen the accuracy of low-methane breeding values. A parallel feed efficiency trial involving around 300 animals will test whether low-methane sheep also convert feed more efficiently, a finding that would strengthen the economic case for farmer adoption beyond emissions alone.

*Why this matters:* Genetics-based methane reduction carries no ongoing per-animal input cost, making it more commercially durable at the farm level than feed additives, and New Zealand is generating the evidence base that other sheep-producing countries will eventually reference.

[Source: AgTech Navigator, "Low-methane sheep genetics fill a gap as mitigation options remain limited"](https://www.agtechnavigator.com/Article/2026/05/12/low-methane-sheep-genetics-fill-a-gap-as-mitigation-options-remain-limited/)

## 5. BioMADE Plans Three US Precision Fermentation Pilot Plants Framed as National Security Infrastructure

Industry consortium BioMADE is building three US pilot-scale precision fermentation facilities: Hayward (California), near Ames (Iowa), and Minneapolis (Minnesota), backed by $21.4 million from the Department of Defense and National Science Foundation. The California site, first to open, will run 4,000-litre tanks with food-grade capability; the Iowa facility, co-located with Iowa State University, will scale to 10,000-litre tanks. The framing as a national security issue reflects a view that US dependence on foreign fermentation infrastructure creates strategic risk comparable to reliance on foreign semiconductor fabs.

*Why this matters:* The US currently lacks the pilot-scale fermentation capacity needed to translate lab proteins into commercial volume; framing this as a national security build-out unlocks defence budgets that commercial investment arguments have so far failed to access.

[Source: FoodNavigator USA, "Precision fermentation: US to open pilot plants as issue of national security"](https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2026/05/11/precision-fermentation-us-to-open-pilot-plants-as-issue-of-national-security/)

## 6. AuX Labs Raises $4M to Scale Animal-Free Casein for Cheese Applications

Canadian startup AuX Labs closed a $4 million seed round in late April, led by NYA Ventures and Nàdarra Ventures, with Verdex Capital, Builders VC, Congruent Ventures, and Bluestein Ventures participating. The company uses precision fermentation to produce casein, the structural protein responsible for melt and stretch in cheese, and obtained self-affirmed GRAS status for its ingredient in April 2025. Rather than designing its protein first and then building production capacity, AuX Labs started from available contract manufacturing infrastructure and reverse-engineered its casein to fit, a move the founders say compresses the timeline to commercial scale.

*Why this matters:* Designing proteins around existing fermentation capacity rather than commissioning bespoke facilities is a materially lower-capital path to market, and the approach is likely to attract more investor interest as the precision dairy sector consolidates.
