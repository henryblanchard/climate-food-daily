## 1. S2G Investments Closes $1 Billion Fund to Back Food, Energy and Ocean Transition

S2G Investments announced the final close of Solutions Fund I on May 12, raising $1 billion from pension funds, funds-of-funds and family offices across North America, Europe, Asia and Australia. The fund targets growth-stage companies in the "missing middle" between early-stage venture and large infrastructure finance, writing cheques of $25 million to $100 million. With $300 million already deployed across ten investments, the portfolio spans food and agriculture, energy infrastructure and ocean systems. S2G says these businesses typically "fall outside traditional capital markets" and the fund often acts as their first institutional partner.

*Why this matters:* The $1 billion close signals that the growth-stage gap in climate-food funding is now attracting dedicated institutional capital rather than occasional crossover investors, which shifts the risk calculus for founders who have cleared proof-of-concept but cannot yet tap infrastructure markets.

## 2. Canada Puts $30 Million into Six Clean Agtech Accelerators

Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada announced on May 8 that it will distribute up to $30 million over two years through six nonprofit organisations: Bioenterprise, CAAIN, the Canadian Food Innovation Network, MaRS Discovery District, Ontario Genomics and Zone Agtech. The programme channels money through specialist intermediaries rather than direct company grants, targeting technologies already advanced enough to move from testing to on-farm deployment. CAAIN, based in Edmonton, receives $6.25 million to extend its smart-farm validation network and help move made-in-Canada technologies through their final commercialisation stage before full market use.

*Why this matters:* Routing public clean-agtech funding through a network of domain-specific accelerators concentrates sector expertise and reduces pipeline risk for late-stage startups; the model is one several other G7 governments are watching as they seek faster routes from farm lab to field deployment.

[Source: Canada.ca, "Government of Canada invests $30 million in six not-for-profit organizations"](https://www.canada.ca/en/agriculture-agri-food/news/2026/05/government-of-canada-invests-30-million-in-six-not-for-profit-organizations-to-help-accelerate-clean-technology-innovation-across-canadian-agriculture.html)

## 3. Octopus Energy Commits $500 Million to Living Carbon's US Reforestation Programme

UK renewable investor Octopus Energy Generation announced on April 30 that it will deploy $500 million into afforestation and reforestation projects developed by Californian land-restoration company Living Carbon, alongside a nearly $13 million direct equity stake in the business. The projects target degraded land including former mining sites and abandoned farmland, with an estimated removal of up to 50 million tonnes of CO2 over 40 years. Pre-committed carbon offtake from Google, Meta and McKinsey through the Symbiosis Coalition provides structured revenue alongside the Octopus capital.

*Why this matters:* Linking long-term institutional infrastructure capital to pre-committed corporate carbon buyers removes the offtake risk that has historically blocked project finance from entering reforestation at scale, offering a replicable financing template for nature-based solutions globally.

[Source: GlobeNewswire, "Living Carbon secures $500m deal with Octopus Energy Generation"](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/04/30/3285028/0/en/Living-Carbon-secures-500m-deal-with-Octopus-Energy-Generation-to-restore-degraded-land-and-remove-CO.html)

## 4. GFI Europe Names Its 2026 Alternative Protein Research Grantees

The Good Food Institute Europe has announced its 2026 cohort of research grant recipients, continuing a programme that has now allocated more than EUR 19 million to researchers in 19 countries. The 2026 cohort spans plant-based, fermentation and cultivated meat projects, including work drawing on European heritage foods, from goulash to distinctive Italian cattle breeds, as proxies for understanding what consumers actually want from alternative proteins. Europe's alternative protein research output has nearly tripled over five years in both published study counts and public funding levels.

*Why this matters:* Academic grant programmes are the least-visible but most durable part of the alt-protein pipeline; the scale of European public co-investment signals policy and regulatory alignment on protein diversification that investors timing market entries cannot afford to ignore.

[Source: GFI Europe, "Meet the 2026 GFI research grantees shaping the future of alternative proteins in Europe"](https://gfieurope.org/blog/meet-the-2026-gfi-research-grantees-shaping-the-future-of-alternative-proteins-in-europe/)

## 5. Regenerative Agriculture Has a Scaling Problem: Nobody Can Agree What It Means

A FoodNavigator analysis published May 12 highlights a core tension holding back regenerative agriculture: 68 of the world's top 100 food companies claim to have a regenerative agriculture strategy, yet there is still no widely accepted standard or definition for the term. The report flags the cost of certification as a specific barrier for smallholder farmers, who risk exclusion from the very systems designed to reward them. Multiple competing certification schemes, including the Rodale-backed Regenerative Organic Certification and the newer Rainforest Alliance regenerative standard, are adding to rather than resolving the definitional fragmentation.

*Why this matters:* Until a credible and widely adopted verification framework exists, corporate "regenerative" claims are essentially unauditable, exposing food brands to greenwashing risk and suppressing the market signal that would otherwise pull more capital into genuinely regenerative supply chains.

[Source: FoodNavigator, "Regenerative agriculture: tensions around trust, value and certification"](https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2026/05/12/regenerative-agriculture-tensions-around-trust-value-and-certification/)

## 6. Agreena Achieves First Agricultural SustainCERT Verification in Hungary and Poland

Danish ag-carbon company Agreena has received SustainCERT verification for a Scope 3 project, issuing 10,964 Verified Impact Units across three European markets, with Hungary and Poland representing the first agricultural VIUs ever issued in those countries. The milestone builds on Agreena's earlier Verra VCS verification of its AgreenaCarbon programme, which issued 2.3 million Verified Carbon Units across more than 1.6 million hectares of regenerative arable farming. The programme uses digital monitoring, reporting and verification combining satellite imagery and AI to track soil carbon changes at field level.

*Why this matters:* Holding both Verra carbon removal credits and SustainCERT impact units lets Agreena serve different corporate buyer segments simultaneously, and first-mover status in eastern European farm markets creates a data and credibility moat that will be hard for competitors to replicate quickly.

[Source: iGrow News, "Agreena Achieves SustainCERT Verification for Scope 3 Carbon Project"](https://igrownews.com/agreena-latest-news/)
